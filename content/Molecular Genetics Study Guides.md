Overall Timeline: 

**1869:** Friedrich Miescher first isolates DNA from white blood cells, calling it "nuclein."

**1930s:** Resurgence of research interest in nucleic acids.

**1944:** Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty demonstrate that DNA is the "transforming principle" that carries genetic information.

**1950:** Erwin Chargaff discovers that the ratios of adenine (A) to thymine (T) and guanine (G) to cytosine (C) are equal in DNA.

**1951:** Rosalind Franklin joins the King’s College group and starts working on X-ray diffraction images of DNA. Maurice Wilkins, at the same institution, obtains high-quality DNA fibers and discovers A and B forms of DNA through X-ray diffraction.

**1951:** discovery of the a-helix and B-sheet, the principal structural features of proteins

**1952:** Rosalind Franklin captures the famous "Photo 51," providing crucial evidence for the helical structure of DNA. Alexander Todd determines the chemical structure of the DNA backbone.

**1953:** James Watson and Francis Crick propose the double helix model for DNA structure, based on Franklin's data (without her knowledge) and Chargaff's rules.

**1957:** Kendrew x ray diffraction of a myoglobin crystal

**1962:** Watson, Crick, and Wilkins are awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for their work on DNA structure.

**1979:** Richard Dickerson and Horace Drew provide the first definitive X-ray crystallographic proof of the DNA double helix.

**1981:** Dickerson carries out a single-crystal structure analysis of B-DNA, using the "Dickerson dodecamer": C-G-C-G-A-A-T-T-C-G-C-G.

**2007:** First two human genomes are sequenced.

**2018:** Development of the iM-specific antibody iMab.

**2019:** Publication of research highlighting the role of Z-DNA and Z-RNA in human diseases.

**2023:** Characterization of iMs in the chromatin context of human cells using CUT&Tag with the iMab antibody.

**2024:** Scientists map i-motifs in purified genomic DNA from human MCF7, U2OS, and HEK293T cells, confirming their widespread presence and potential regulatory role.
