
So, my downstairs neighbor has a very large dog that spend a lot of time in our shared backyard. I can be lethally injured by a dog of that size if it were to jump on me. Could I push the landlord to require the other tenant to use a leash? 

If you have concerns about your safety due to a neighbor's dog in a shared living environment, there are several steps you can take, and you do have rights that can be addressed, primarily under the auspices of ensuring a safe living environment. While specific laws and regulations can vary by location, here are some general approaches you might consider:

1. **Communicate Your Concerns**: The first step could be to discuss your concerns directly with your neighbor if you feel comfortable doing so. They may not be aware of the impact their pet is having on you and might be willing to take measures to address the situation.

2. **Review Your Lease Agreement**: Check your lease agreement for any clauses related to pets, shared spaces, and tenant conduct. Some leases specify rules about pets in shared areas, including leash requirements.

3. **Contact Your Landlord or Property Management**: If direct communication doesn’t resolve the issue, or if you prefer not to approach your neighbor, inform your landlord or property management of your concerns in writing. Emphasize the safety aspect and your fear of being injured. Landlords have a responsibility to ensure a safe environment for all tenants. You can request that the landlord enforce, or if necessary, establish a rule requiring all dogs to be on a leash in shared spaces.

4. **Legal Protections and Reasonable Accommodations**: Under the Fair Housing Act (FHA), you may be entitled to reasonable accommodations due to a disability or to ensure your safety and enjoyment of your living space. If your concern about the dog relates to a disability or a condition that could be exacerbated by an encounter with the dog, mentioning this in your communication to the landlord could strengthen your request. The landlord may then be required to make accommodations, which could include enforcing a leash policy in shared areas.

5. **Local Ordinances**: Many cities and municipalities have leash laws that require dogs to be on a leash when not on the owner's private property. You can research local ordinances in your area regarding leash requirements. If such laws exist and are not being followed, you can bring this to the attention of your landlord and, if necessary, report violations to local authorities.

6. **Documentation**: Keep a record of all incidents, as well as any communication with your neighbor and your landlord. This documentation can be important if the issue escalates and you need to prove that you have made reasonable efforts to resolve the situation.

If the situation does not improve and you feel your safety is at risk, you may need to seek legal advice. A lawyer can provide guidance based on your specific circumstances and local laws, and help you understand your rights and options for further action.

Remember, it's important to approach this situation in a manner that seeks resolution and maintains a peaceful living environment for all parties involved.
