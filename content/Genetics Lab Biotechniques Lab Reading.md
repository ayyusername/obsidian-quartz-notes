**Basics of DNA Extraction:** DNA was first isolated from the nuclei of human white blood cells by the Swiss physician and biologist, Friedrich Miescher, in 1869 using various salt solutions and purification and precipitation using basic and acidic solutions. Miescher noted that the precipitate _nuclein_ contained nitrogen and phosphorous (Dahm 2008). He identified _nuclein_ as a molecule with the potential to be responsible for inheritance. There are three basic methods of DNA extraction. Optimal method selection depends upon the type of sample that DNA is being extracted from, the level of contamination that must to be removed to carryout downstream molecular analyses, and the cost and equipment required for the extraction.

The aim in a DNA extraction is to isolate DNA in the nucleus or cytoplasm from other cellular components. Many methods and technologies are available to isolate genomic DNA. The choice of method depends on the type of sample (for example soil, tissue, cell culture), the quantity and molecular weight of DNA required, time and expense. Considerations in DNA extraction also include avoiding fragmentation of DNA by shearing and preventing the action of endogenous nucleases such as DNase that catalyzes the hydrolytic cleavage of phosphodiester linkages in the DNA backbone, thus degrading DNA.

**B. Restriction Digestion** 

Restriction Digestion of wild type and mutant _Arabidopsis_ DNA using McrBC: A methylation dependent enzyme (McrBC) is used in the Detecting Epigenetic Methylation in _Arabidopsis thaliana_ experiment in order to detect differenced in DNA methylation between the wild type and mutant phenotypes. This section introduces you to some background information on restriction digestion that supplements the lecture materials.

**Restriction Endonucleases:** Restriction-modification systems are one mechanism used by bacteria to prevent or restrict infection by bacterial viruses (bacteriophage or phage). Restriction modification systems have two components, a **restriction endonuclease** (**restriction enzyme or RE**) that recognizes specific DNA sequences and cleaves at or near those recognition sites, and a **DNA methylase** (**modification system**) that adds methyl groups (–CH3) to the sites recognized by the restriction endonuclease (RE).  Methylation of recognition sites in its genome prevents cleavage by the RE, which would result in death of the host.  A single bacterial species generally has multiple restriction modification systems. A phage that previously infected a different species will not have protective methyl groups at the sites recognized by the REs in other species, and the phage’s DNA genome will be cleaved when entering a cell, preventing successful infection of other species. A phage that previously infected the same species will have methyl groups at the sites recognized by the species’ REs, preventing cleavage, and allowing infection.

**Restriction Enzyme Modification System Classes**

- **Class I** systems are comprised of three subunits encoded by a single gene/operon:  A subunit that recognizes specific DNA sequences, a subunit that methylates those sequences, and the RE subunit that cleaves the DNA at that site.  None of the subunits will work unless all three are together and both ATP and S-adenosylmethionine (-CH3 donor) are present.  Recognition sequences are relatively long, with no symmetry, and the RE cleaves randomly near, but not in, recognition sites.  Cleave is at sites remote from the recognition site.
- **Class II** systems have two enzymes encoded by separate genes that work independently of each other.  The methylase and RE recognize identical short sequences, usually with reverse symmetry (palindromic), and the RE cleaves **at a specific site**, **in a specific way**, in or near the recognition site.  No ATP is needed by either enzyme to function.  The methylase recognizes nucleotides in the same sequence, blocking cleavage by the RE. Cleavage is at a short specific distance from a recognition site.
- **Class III** systems are like Class I in requiring ATP and S-adenosylmethionine, and like Class II systems in having two enzymes and recognizing short sequences with inverse symmetry.  Class III REs cleave a defined distance on either side of the recognition sequence, rather than in the recognition sites.  Consequently, no defined ends are created. Cleavage is at a short distance from a recognition site.
- **Class IV** systems target modified DNA (such as methylated).
- **Class V** systems use guide RNAs to target sequences. The cas9-gRNA complex is used in CRISPR gene editing techniques.

**Naming Restriction Endonucleases**

Restriction enzymes are named based on their genus and species, and sometimes strains of origin.  For example, the _Eco_ in _Eco_RI is from the first letter of the genus and the first two letters of the species name _Escherichia coli_.  The three letters are italicized by convention because genus and species names are italicized. The R comes from strain RY 13, and is not italicized because, by convention, strain names are not italicized.  The “I” indicates that _Eco_RI is the first restriction enzyme identified in _E. coli_.  Another example is _Bam_HI, which recognizes GGATCC, and is the first restriction enzyme identified in _Bacillus amyloliquefaciens_ strain H.

**Recognition sites and cleavage**

Class II REs are considered the most useful in molecular biology because the RE is easily purified from the methylase, no ATP is needed for cleavage activity, **and cleavage always occurs at the same location and in the same way** relative to the recognition site.  Hundreds of Class II REs are commercially available, each recognizing a sequence that is 4, 6, or 8 bp long. As would be expected, REs that recognize a 4 bp sequence, such as AATT, cleave more often than enzymes that have 8 bp recognition sites, such as AAAATTTT.  On average, it is the probability of AATT ( ¼ x ¼ x ¼ x ¼ ) versus the probability of AAAATTTT ( ¼ x ¼ x ¼ x ¼  x ¼ x ¼ x ¼ x ¼) in determining the expected frequency of cleavage by an enzyme.

**Restriction Mapping**

The discovery of restriction enzymes that cleaved at defined sites in a genome or region of DNA allowed the creation of physical maps of DNA.  Genetic work prior to the use of restriction enzymes allowed the ordering and determination of genetic distance between genes and sites in genes based on recombination frequencies and the creation of very useful genetic maps, but not the physical structure of genes and chromosomes.  The use of restriction enzymes allowed the creation of “restriction maps” of regions that could be used to identify regions similar to or different from each other, identify the sites of mutations and chromosome rearrangements, detect repeated DNA sequences, and assist with developing cloning strategies.

**Restriction Enzyme Reaction Conditions**

Restriction enzymes need approximately physiological conditions (theirs) with respect to temperature, pH, salt concentrations, divalent cations, and the presence of other proteins to recognize and to cleave DNA efficiently. To provide the appropriate salt and divalent cation, concentrations, and pH, one tenth of the total final volume of a 10´ concentrated “buffer” solution is used with commercially available enzymes, comparable to the 10´ Buffer supplied with _Taq_ polymerase.  Restriction enzymes require a wide variety of conditions, but common 10´ Buffers contain: 0-1.5 M NaCl, 100 mM MgCl2 and 100 mM Tris (pH 7.4 - 8.0).  The NaCl provides salt, the MgCl2 provides Mg++ needed as a high-energy cofactor and the Tris establishes and maintains an approximately neutral pH.  Highly purified Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) is often added to reactions; BSA is a cheap, non-enzymatic protein thought to bind to contaminants that would interfere with the activity of the restriction enzyme.  Optimal temperatures for restriction enzymes range from 20oC to 65oC, with the vast majority cleaving optimally at 37oC.

**Units**

The ability of an enzyme to cleave DNA is measured in terms of "Units."  One Unit is defined as the amount of enzyme required to cleave 1 µg of a benchmark DNA, usually pBR322 DNA (a plasmid), in 1 hour under optimal conditions. 

 **C. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**

PCR will be used in the Detecting Epigenetic Methylation in _Arabidopsis thaliana_ experiment to differentiate between methylated and non-methylated DNA after the DNA is extracted and a restriction digest is performed. The difference in phenotypes will be detectable by analyzing the PCR products, which allows for you to detect the underlying genetic mechanism that explains the differences in phenotypic variation between wild type and mutant _Arabidopsis_ samples with respect to flowering time. 

**Polymerase chain reaction (PCR)** is a molecular process for synthesizing millions of copies of one or a few original strands of DNA.  There are main components for synthesis of a DNA molecule: dNTPs, template DNA, primers, and an enzyme (_Taq_ polymerase).

PCR is a molecular technique that allows scientists to target specific regions of a nucleic acid for a variety of applications from studying evolution, biodiversity, populations, and to research gene function and identification. The revolutionary method was developed by Kary Mullis in the 1980s and is based on using the ability of a specific [DNA polymerase](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/probe/docs/glossary#DNApol "Definition of DNA polymerase")

[Links to an external site.](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/probe/docs/glossary#DNApol "Definition of DNA polymerase") to synthesize new strand of DNA complementary to the ‘target’ template strand. Kary Mullis was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his invention of PCR in 1993.

A single PCR reaction involves three main temperature-dependent steps: (1) Denaturation of double stranded DNA (dsDNA) to single stranded DNA (ssDNA), (2) Annealing of primers, and (3) Elongation or synthesis of the polynucleotide strand. DNA polymerase can add a nucleotide only onto a preexisting 3'-OH group because nucleotides are bound by a phosphodiester bond on the 3’ end of a DNA molecule. The reaction requires a [primer](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/probe/docs/glossary#primer "Definition of primer")

[Links to an external site.](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/probe/docs/glossary#primer "Definition of primer") to which the first nucleotide can be added so that the remaining nucleotides can be added,  synthesizing a growing strand from a forward primer location to a reverse primer location on the target genome sequence. Primers make it possible to delineate a specific region of template sequence that the researcher wants to amplify.

Central to the technique is that the reaction is produces amplified regions exponentially, resulting in billions of copies (called amplicons) at the end of the reaction. With each thermocycle of amplification, the number of targeted amplicons doubles, resulting in the accumulation of billions of [amplicons](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/probe/docs/glossary#amplicon "Definition of amplicon")

[Links to an external site.](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/probe/docs/glossary#amplicon "Definition of amplicon") that can then be observed using techniques such as gel electrophoresis.

In this course, you will use PCR in a variety of applications. In the study of detecting DNA methylation in _Arabidopsis_, you will be using PCR to differentiate between a methylated and a non-methylated gene. In doing so, you will be observing the difference in phenotypes and then observing the underlying genetic differences that explain the phenotypic variation in the timing of flowering development in _Arabidopsis_. 

**The Main Steps of PCR:**

**Denaturation:** at a high enough temperature (depending on the length and nucleotide composition), double-stranded DNA unwinds and the hydrogen bonds that hold the two strands together weaken and finally break.  The critical temperature at which the DNA melts, _Tm_, is defined as the temperature at which half of the DNA strands are in the single-stranded (ssDNA) state.

**Annealing:** when a heated solution of single-stranded DNA is allowed to cool, in the course of the normal random molecular motion complementary strands (primers or complete strands) eventually bump into each other and re-form double-stranded DNA.

**Elongation:** beginning where a primer is base-paired with the template, polymerase adds complementary DNA nucleotides to the 3' end of the newly synthesized polynucleotide strand.  When the end of the strand is reached Taq polymerase adds a final adenine residue (3’A overhang) to the 3´-end of both strands of DNA molecules then falls off the strand and begins again when it alights upon a new primer-template hybrid.  A rule of thumb for elongation is to allow 1 minute per 1,000 bp..

**The components of PCR:**

**_Taq_ DNA polymerase:**  a thermostable (at £95oC) enzyme that synthesizes long chains (polymers) of nucleic acids from the 5’g3’ direction and also has 3'g5' exonuclease (proofreading) activity.  The enzyme only can add nucleotides from an existing area where a primer is base-paired with a template.  _Taq_ Polymerase originally was derived from the hot spring bacteria, _Thermus_ _aquaticus_, and now is produced industrially by being cloned into _E_. _coli_.

**Template DNA:** purified double-stranded (linear or circular) DNA (dsDNA) added to a PCR reaction, made single-stranded by heating, then copied millions of times over the course of repeated heating and cooling cycles.

**Oligonucleotide primers:** Short single-stranded stretches of 2’-deoxyribonucleotides that bind to specific DNA (or RNA) sequences on a single-stranded template and serve as the origin of replication of the remaining complementary sequence via a polymerase. Primers are identified as Forward and Reverse based on the 5’ to 3’ sequence of DNA template that is targeted for amplification. Primers are designed to be between 18 and 30 bases in length, however, the most important considerations are the primer pair Tm value (melting temperature) and specificity. Primers should also not be self-complimentary.

**Nucleotides: dNTPs: (**deoxyribose nucleotide triphosphates, nucleotide bases G,A,T,C) that are the energy source for PCR (high energy phosphate bonds) and the building blocks of DNA.  During PCR, they are added to the growing chain in a 5’g3’ direction. Nucleotides are added to the growing polypeptide chain at the 3’ end via a phosphodiester bond.

**Thermal cycler:** laboratory instrument that holds the PCR reaction mixtures and raises-lowers the temperature in discrete programmed steps for variable amounts of time.

**Master Mix:**Rather than adding each component to each PCR reaction separately (which leaves many opportunities for human error), scientists use a “**Master Mix**” that contains all the components common to all reactions.  Our Master Mix will contain the **Buffer** (Tris, Salt, Mg++), **dNTPs**, and **_Taq_ polymerase**.  To optimize a PCR reaction, some component concentrations may need to be altered.

**Melting temperature (Tm):** The optimal melting temperature of the primers is based on typical cycling and reaction conditions and the optimum temperature for PCR enzyme function. A range of 60–64°C is optimal, with an ideal temperature of 62°C. Ideally, the melting temperatures of the 2 primers should not differ by more than 2°C in order for both primers to bind simultaneously and efficiently amplify the product. Annealing temperature (Ta): The annealing temperature chosen for PCR relies directly on length and composition of the primers. This temperature should be no more than 5°C below the Tm of your primers.

One consequence of having Ta too low is that one or both primers will anneal to sequences other than the intended target because internal single-base mismatches or partial annealing may be tolerated. This can lead to nonspecific PCR amplification and will consequently reduce the yield of the desired product. Conversely, if Ta is too high, reaction efficiency may be reduced because the likelihood of primer annealing is reduced significantly. Optimal annealing temperatures will result in the highest product yield with the correct amplicon.

GC content: Design your assay so that the GC content is 35–65%, with an ideal content of 50%, which allows complexity while still maintaining a unique sequence. Primer sequences should not contain regions of 4 or more consecutive G residues.

**Thermal cycling**

When performing PCR on cells, an initial 2-minute 94oC step is used to fully denature the lengthy template DNA and activate the _Taq_ polymerase.  In subsequent cycles, a brief 30-second 94oC step denatures the shorter synthesized DNA strands.  The next lower annealing step allows both primers to anneal to complementary sequences. The annealing temperature is based on the _Tm_s of the two primers, usually 1-5o C below the average _Tm_ of the two primers. The 72oC step allows _Taq_ polymerase to elongate from the 3’ ends of both annealed primers Forward and Reverse on opposite template strands.  A final 3-minute 72oC incubation allows _Taq_ polymerase to complete extension of any prematurely terminated strands and ensure that all 3’ ends have a terminal adenine.  Thereafter, the instrument is programmed to hold at 15oC, which is a low enough temperature that further enzymatic activity (synthesis or degradation) is negligible.

**D. Gel Electrophoresis**

**Electrophoresis for DNA size fractionation**

One way to determine the size of DNA fragments is to use electrophoresis, a form of chromatography, in which an electrical field is used to cause DNA fragments to move or migrate through a solid matrix.  Common matrices are formed with agarose, a highly refined version of the polysaccharide polymer used to make bacterial plates solid, or with polyacrylamide.  Both agarose and polyacrylamide form matrices with small pores, the higher the concentration of either polymer, the smaller the pores.  The pore sizes in agarose gels range from 200 to 400 nm, and in polyacrylamide, pore sizes range from 19 to 150 nm.  Agarose is best suited for size fractionating and resolving DNA fragments between 100 and 60,000 bp and polyacrylamide is best suited for size fractionating and resolving DNA fragments between 20 and 2,000 bases.

DNA size fractionation using agarose gel electrophoresis requires an agarose gel with wells at one end in which to place DNA samples, a neutral pH, a salt-containing “buffer” to conduct electrical current between electrodes through the buffer and matrix, and a plastic box or chamber to contain all the necessary elements.  A power supply creates the necessary electrical field so that negatively charged DNA migrates toward the positive (+) pole, or anode, due to the negative charge of the phosphate groups that comprise part of the sugar-phosphate backbone.  The higher the voltage applied, the faster DNA molecules migrate (and the more heat that is generated).  Smaller fragments travel more quickly through the agarose matrix than larger fragments.

**Theories of DNA migration**

There are several theories regarding how DNA moves through agarose and polyacrylamide, which must explain why smaller DNA fragments migrate faster than larger DNA fragments.  The distance that a DNA fragment migrates is in a **logarithmic** rather than a linear relationship to its mass and length due the constant charge-to-mass ratio for each base/base pair.  The **Ogston Sieving model** envisions that DNA molecules form blobs that pass through the pores in the matrix akin to a pinball game with different size balls, gates and holes. The larger the DNA molecular, the larger the “blob” and the fewer pores the DNA molecule can pass through, resulting in smaller molecules migrating faster.  The **Reptation model** envisions that the DNA molecules behave as worms or snakes sliding through a series of tunnels (pores) (Figure 1).  Due to increased electrostatic interactions, longer DNA molecules interact more with the matrix, slowing their migration relative to smaller molecules.  **A third model** envisions that DNA forms

several conformations, including blobs, partial blobs, lines heading with the current (worms/snakes), U shapes, lines with hooks on their ends, and lines moving perpendicular to their axes.  What then occurs is that many molecules get snagged or tangled with the matrix and are slowly pulled off by the attraction to the anode.  Because longer molecules have more opportunities to snag, they migrate more slowly through the matrix than shorter molecules. It is likely that all three models are accurate to a degree.

**Electrophoresis buffer**

The pH rises dramatically during electrophoresis which can detrimentally affect DNA and RNA structure and movement.  Thus, the liquid medium for electrophoresis is “**buffered**” to prevent radical change in pH.  Electrophoretic buffers have a **pH buffering** agent such as **Tris**, a **salt**, and the preservative **EDTA** (also used as a chelating agent in **Stop Mix**).  The buffer used in Exercise 5 is **1 X TAE** [0.04 M Tris-HCl, 20 mM Na acetate, 1 mM EDTA (pH 8.0)], which is **present in the gel** **and** in the **buffer** to conduct current between the electrodes and through the agarose gel.  It is critical that **diH2O** **NOT** be used to create a gel or to fill an electrophoresis chamber because pure diH2O does not conduct electricity well and creates a concentration gradient when one part of the system is diH2O and the other is 1X TAE.  TAE has the desirable features of being inexpensive, easy to store in diluted form, the ability to withstand high temperatures, and it can be reused once.  Disadvantages include that acetate ions can interfere with downstream applications if DNA is excised from gels for enzymatic treatments with restriction enzymes, DNA ligases, or DNA polymerases.

**Electrophoresis chamber**

Agarose gel electrophoresis often is performed using an open cast **gel** placed in a horizontal electrophoresis chamber with the gel submerged in the buffer.  To save time and reduce toxic waste, we however will use a precast gel in a cassette called an E-Gel (Figure 2).  The E-Gel is created the same way an open gel is cast, by pouring molten agarose into a plastic form and while molten, inserting a comb into the liquid agarose.  When the comb is removed, the teeth leave behind depressions called “**wells**.”

It is critical when loading samples into the wells to **NOT** insert the pipette tip through the bottom of the well, but rather, just break the surface of the buffer above the well and let the sample sink into the well.  The two dyes included in STOP Mix are **Xylene cyanol** (**XC**), which migrates at approximately the same speed through agarose as 3,000-3,500 bp DNA fragments, and **bromophenol blue** (**BPB**), which migrates at approximately the same speed as a 300-500 bp DNA fragment (depending on buffer and agarose concentration).  Monitoring the migration of the two dyes makes it possible to turn off current through the gel at the appropriate time based on the expected sizes of DNA fragments of interest.  In this Exercise, DNA fragments of 500 bp and larger are of interest, so it is **important to make sure that the BPB dye front does not reach the anode end of the gel** before stopping electrophoresis.  

Once all samples are “**loaded**” into wells, the E-Gel will be inserted into the power source which connects electrical leads between the cassette and the power supply, and the power supply is turned on to create an electric field in the cassette.  When the electric field is activated, negatively charged DNA will migrate toward the **anode** (+/red pole).  **Never come in contact with buffer or metal parts of electrodes during electrophoresis as this is a dangerous shock hazard.**

**Visualizing double-stranded DNA**

Many stains are used to identify where DNA is located in an agarose gel following electrophoresis including silver, ethidium bromide (EtBr), SYBRgreen, SYBRsafe, GelRed, and other stains.  A quick, inexpensive, highly sensitive, yet **highly toxic** method is the use of **ethidium bromide**.  Because of its shape and hydrophobicity, EtBr **intercalates** between base paired nucleotides in double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) and becomes more concentrated inside base-paired DNA than in the buffer and surrounding gel (Figure 3).  EtBr is the stain included in the E-Gel cassette.  It is a potent mutagen (causes changes in DNA), carcinogen (causes cancer), and taratogen (causes defects in embryos and fetuses) because it intercalates into dsDNA.  Its presence interferes with transcription and replication.  During transcription and replication, the RNA and DNA polymerases will either stop completely or add an extra nucleotide at locations where EtBr is intercalated, resulting in frame shift mutations.  It is essential that all safety procedures noted be followed for your safety, the safety of others, and the safety of the environment.  Although many labs still use EtBr, it is toxic to us and the environment and there are less toxic alternatives.  In cases where we make our own gels (Ex 5B-D), we will use SYBRsafe instead of EtBr.  A data sheet showing comparable characteristics of DNA stains is included in the Bb folder for this lab.

To visualize where double-stranded DNA is located following staining, a stained gel is illuminated with either UV light (for EtBr) or blue light (SYBRsafe), causing the intercalated compounds to fluoresce at a lower energy and longer wavelength than the original light (**Beer’s Law**).  **Always wear UV-blocking goggles when working with UV sources to protect your eyes.**  Using a camera equipped with a filter to block the UV or blue source’s wavelength while allowing the wavelength emitted by the fluorescence of EtBr or SYBRsafe to pass through, it is possible to visualize and capture an image of the DNA in the gel.  Because of the shape of the wells, DNA fragments of the same size will form “**bands**” of fluorescence in the gel (Figure 4).  Different size fragments will migrate different distances from the well.  The more different sizes present, the more bands detected in each “**lane**.”

**Size standards**

To determine the sizes of DNA fragments following electrophoresis, it is necessary to include a sample with DNA fragments of known size in the range expected for the unknowns.  These are called **DNA size standards** or **DNA ladders**.  Because we are expecting fragments of between 3,000 and 500 bp, we are using the **E-gel 1KB Plus Ladder** (catalog no. 10488-090), which has 12 dsDNA fragments from 100–12,000 bp (Figure 5). In any electrophoresis, it often is not possible to distinguish all of the bands.  If small fragments are of interest, large fragments in the size standard may not be separated sufficiently to **resolve** individual bands.  If large fragments are of interest, small fragments in the ladder may have passed out the (+) end of the gel into the tank.  Notice that some bands are brighter than others (more DNA of that size) and that there are some odd sizes of bands in the 1Kb Plus Ladder, which promotes easy discrimination of doublets.

**Creating a standard curve**

To create a standard curve based on a size standard, the distance from the leading edge of the well (origin) to the center of each visible known fragment is measured in millimeters.  This can be done several ways, but for class, **a clear ruler will be placed on the picture of the gel to make measurements.**  To plot size _versus_ migration distance, two methods are possible.  When using standard graph paper, the horizontal (x) axis is migration distance (measured in mm from the leading edge of the well) and the vertical (y) axis is log10 of the fragment size in base pairs (bp).  For example, the log10 of a 1,000 bp fragment is 3 on the vertical axis.  For each visible DNA fragment/fluorescent band, migration distance is plotted versus log10 of size in base pairs.  If semi-log paper is used, the process is modified slightly wherein the horizontal axis is distance of migration (in mm), but the vertical axis simply has the actual size in base pairs.  After all the points for the distinguishable DNA fragments in the size standard are plotted (100-3,000 bp), **it is sufficiently accurate for BIOZ310 to connect the points with a line to create the standard curve** (Figure 6).

**Use of the standard curve to estimate the sizes of DNA fragments**

To determine the sizes of unknowns using a standard curve plotted on standard graph paper, measure the distance from the leading edge of the well to the middle of the DNA fragments of unknown size migrated (only for DNA samples in the same gel as the size standard).  For each unknown fragment, draw a vertical line up from the point on the x-axis that corresponds to the migration distance (shown as dotted line on Figure 6).  Where the vertical line intersects the standard curve, draw a horizontal line to the y-axis (**extrapolation**).  Where the horizontal line intersects the Y-axis is the size, in log10, of the unknown fragment. 

To convert the size of the unknown DNA fragments to base 10, raise 10 to the _n_th power, where _n_ is the point where the horizontal line intersects the Y-axis.  For example, to determine the size of a fragment with a y-intercept of 3, 103 = 1,000 bp.  Plotting and extrapolation is similar when using semi-log paper except that there is no need to raise 10 to the y-axis value.  A fragment that migrated 25 mm would be ~ 900 bp and a fragment that migrated 29 mm would be ~ 500 bp.  **There is a minimum of a 5% error on all size estimates,** and more error with shorter gels or shorter electrophoresis times, including those used in this Exercise.  The objective of using semi-log paper or converting to log10 is to allow the plotting of a nearly straight line when “connecting the dots.”  Computers are now routinely used to plot best-fit lines, to calculate coefficient of determination (r2) values, and to perform extrapolations to determine the sizes of unknowns.  As shown in Figure 6, although the points in the center of the distribution form a nearly straight line, it is common for the largest and smallest fragments to deviate from the logarithmic relationship between size and migration, indicating that a more complex relationship is occurring.

**E. DNA Sanger Sequencing**

**Sanger Sequencing**

Maxim and Gilbert (1977) devised the first method to determine the sequence of nucleotides on a DNA backbone using chemical digestions and radioactive nucleotides.  The process was effective, but expensive, labor intensive, slow and filled with health hazards including caustic and radioactive chemicals.  Later in 1977, Sanger _et al_. (1977) published a sequencing technique based on using DNA polymerase to synthesize DNA in the presence of nucleotides lacking a 3’-OH group, which could be incorporated into a new backbone due to presence of 5’ phosphate, but which could not be extended from due to the lack of the 3’-OH group required by DNA polymerase. 

![](https://virginiacommonwealth.instructure.com/courses/107284/files/12576178/preview)

**Fig. 7.** Sanger Chain termination DNA sequence analysis.  From Weaver (2005).

These nucleotides are called **dideoxynucleotides** because they also lack the 2’-OH (because DNA is deoxyribonucleic acid).  This method, called the **Chain Termination** or **Dideoxy Sequencing**, revolutionized the field due to the ease, ability to scale up, and lack of dangerous chemicals other than radioactive nucleotides.  Gilbert and Sanger shared the 1980 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for their contributions towards determining the sequence of DNA.  The basic premise for Chain Termination sequencing (Figure 1) is that reactions contain template, one oligomer primer, deoxynucleotides (dNTPs), dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs), buffer (Tris, salt, and MgCl2), and _Taq_ polymerase.  As in PCR, samples are heated to denature the template and cooled to allow a single oligomer primer to anneal to the ssDNA template.  _Taq_ Polymerase begins adding dNTPs and a smaller proportion of ddNTPs.  Because of the excess of dNTPs, at most points the polymerase will incorporate a dNTP and continue past the site.  But when a ddNTP is added, **the polymerase cannot continue**, terminating the chain (giving rise to the name Chain Termination Sequencing).

Because these fragments differ by 1 bp increments, they must be separated using poly-acrylamide.  Visualization of terminated fragments originally relied on the incorporation of radioactive P32 and autoradiography using X-ray film.  Today, chain-termination fragments are generated using fluorescently labeled ddNTPs where **each ddNTP has a different dye** (Dye Terminator Sequencing).  Because each dideoxy fluoresces at a different wavelength, it is possible to combine all the ddNTPs into a single reaction tube and distinguish chains ending in each dideoxy by the wavelength emitted when illuminated with a strong laser.  Using capillary polyacrylamide electrophoresis, as labeled fragments in a capillary move across the path of a laser beam, they fluoresce and a camera detects and records the colors, which correspond to the four ddNTPs.  A computer connected to the detector determines or “**calls”** the base sequence based on the size and spacing of the fluorescent peaks.  Because all fragments started from the same primer, all had the same 5’ end, but different 3’ ends based on where the chain was terminated (where the ddNTP was added).  Combining the information from the four colors, it is possible to determine the overall 5’®3’ sequence (Figure 2).  The data we receive following Sanger Sequencing consist of a text file of the nucleotides called by the computer algorithm and a **chromatogram**, a.k.a. **electropherogram**, for each sample showing “**peaks**” representing the fluorescence of each terminated fragment collected by the detector at that time as that ssDNA fragment passed through the laser/detector window.  

![](https://virginiacommonwealth.instructure.com/courses/107284/files/12576261/preview)

**Fig. 8.** Comparison of fluorescent Sanger Chain termination DNA Sequencing with radioactive analysis.  From https://www3.appliedbiosystems.com/ cms/groups/mcb_support/documents/generaldocuments/ cms_041003.pdf

Throughout the 1990s-2000s, Sanger sequencing, combined with capillary electrophoresis (polyacrylamide is inserted into hair-thin capillary tubes) and robotics, allowed hundreds to **thousands** of samples to be analyzed **per day** at a maximum size per sample of around **500 bp**.  Current methods (2010s) based on populating beads with single DNA templates and then washing nucleotides successively over the templated beads allow us to collect **millions** of reads **per hour** from a sequencer. 

These “**nextgen**” sequencing platforms utilize fluorescence or pH and generate reads that are nearly as long as Sanger sequencing (~400 bp currently is the maximum length).  The newest sequencing platform uses nanopores filled with proteins that grab intact full-length DNA molecules and wratchet them one bp at a time through the pores, generating **thousands** of DNA reads **per hour** that are >10,000 bp.  Nanopore sequencing data are derived solely from minute differences in charge of the four nucleotides, eliminating the need for fluorochrome labeling, lasers, and pH detectors.  We choose Sanger sequencing because we wish to know the sequences of individual amplicons.  Another researcher may choose nextgen or nanopore sequencing because they wish to know the sequences of thousands or millions of DNA fragments that are in a mixture.

**DNA Analysis using the U.S. National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI)**

You will be using a central government managed research database to collect data and research material for your project on epigenetics in _Arabidopsis_. Because of the vast amounts of genetic data collected around the world, there has been an explosion in sequence data that are available to the research community. 

The primary source in the U.S. is **GenBank**, which is part of **NCBI** and administered by **National Institutes of Health** (**NIH**).  NCBI is the repository for sequences published in papers, complete genome sequences for many animal, plant, fungal, and bacterial organisms, organelle genomes, sequence variation data for populations, and environmental DNA accessions.  The first complete genome sequence for a species is termed the **reference genome** and subsequent sequences for parts or all of a genome are compared to that reference.  GenBank also includes protein sequences, RNA sequences, RNA expression patterns, and methylation and chromatin patterns.  NCBI includes tools for searching and comparing sequences to each other, as well as **PubMed** for searching for articles by author, title, topic, and key words.  In this exercise, the **Basic Local Alignment Search Tool** (**BLAST**) tool will be used to search sequences housed in GenBank for biologically meaningful relationships to the sequences derived from our PCR products.
