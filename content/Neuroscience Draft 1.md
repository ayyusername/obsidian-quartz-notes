Rapidly following threshold depolarization via microelectrode pulse current, voltage gated Ca2+ ion channels lining the nerve presynaptic terminal membrane open. This cytoplasmic Ca2+ influx triggers two processes: vesicle release and vesicle fusion. Fully loaded vesicles warehouse together, coupled via synapsin to actin-framed flotilla adjacent to the presynaptic terminal. Ca binds cytosolic CaMKII to dephosphorylate synapsin, triggering vesicle uncoupling. Sufficiently proximal to the presynaptic membrane, vesicle-coating vSNARE proteins, notably synaptobrevin, complex membrane bound tSNAREs SNAP-25 and syntaxin to dock the vesicle on the nerve terminal. vSNARE Synaptotagmin serves as a Ca2+ sensor and, once signaled, conformationally changes to bind both the SNARE complex and presynaptic membrane, deforming the membranes sufficiently close to vault repulsive hydrophobic forces and release neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft via membrane fusion pore. In this case glutamate is the released neurotransmitter, which then either binds a postsynaptic receptor or is extracted from the synapse.

Synaptic extraction occurs rapidly via Excitatory Amino Acid Transporters lining both glia and the nerve terminal to promote discreet neurotransmitter signaling, prevent glutamate driven excitotoxicity, and pursue metabolic favorability. Glial glutamate catalyzes to glutamine via glutamine synthetase prior to export via SN1. Extracellular glutamine is then transported into nerve terminal, landing ultimately in the mitochondrial matrix for hydrolysis into glutamate via glutaminase prior to packaging via vesicular glutamate transporter VGLUT. Glutaminase is translated in the neuronal soma and diffuses along the axon to the terminal bouton for local neurotransmitter synthesis and packaging. 

Glutamate can bind both metabotrophic receptors and ionotrophic receptors. mGlu receptors are classical G-protein coupled receptors involved in both synaptic plasticity and longer-term signal cascades. Glutamate ion channels, including NMDA, AMPA, and Kainate classes, are involved in changing post-synaptic membrane potential by regulating the influx of Na+, K+, and in the case of AMPA Ca2+. Each bound ion channel serves to bring the post-synaptic membrane potential further or closer to threshold, and mGluR serve to either modify ionotrophic behavior or influence second messenger cascades.
